Drives
Belt (indirect) driven fans are the most common and have the advantages that they are readily accessible for maintenance, require simple maintenance, easy to adjust the speed for the system design, and low stress on the motor. The main disadvantage is the efficiency loss of 3% to 5% compared to direct drive. Synchronous drive belts can improve system performance by 2%. Direct drive fans can provide higher efficiency and have a simpler design compared to belt driven fans. However, may have increased stress on the motor and bearings and most applications require a method for speed control like a variable frequency drive. Refer the Applications of Fans in Commercial HVAC equipment report by Carrier for more details.
Motors
Most HVAC system motors are AC induction motors with single phase motors for small systems (< 5 hp) and three phase motors for larger systems. The most common single phase motor are permanent split capacitance and some shaded pole (smaller motors and very low performance). Speed control and improved part load efficiency can be achieved with the addition of variable frequency drives (VFD); however, VFDs add a 3% to 5% efficiency penalty. More efficient motor options include permanent magnet motors (e.g. electrically commutated [EC]) and switched reluctance motors. EC and other permanent magnet motors are widely available for small (< 1hp) motors and are starting to become more available for midsized motors (2-5 hp). Switched reluctance motors have the advantage of not using expensive rare earth materials and recent advances are overcoming speed and vibration control challenges.
