Fan Tune-up Measures
Measures Discriptions

Inefficiencies in fan systems can be attributed to faults related to the fan equipment or the system as a whole. The measures listed below are therefore classified as fan measures and system measures. Each measure listed below has a brief description and a link to a reference for more details.

In applying any of the measures, safety is the foremost concern. The recommended measures recommended should be implemented only by trained and certified personnel.

Fan Measures

Fan performance can be compromised by component problems and can be corrected with proper maintenance and or replacement.

Fan Maintenance (ASHRAE Standard 180-2012)
Measure Level of Effort Frequency
Check belt tension and wear
Proper belt tension is required to prevent noise, vibration and wearing of bearings. Few methods of measuring belt tension are deflection, frequency and using tension-finding devices. After checking the belt tension, tighten or loosen the belt as needed.1

Belt loses tension as it starts to wear and the power transmission efficiency goes down.2 Common indicators of belt wear are cracks and fraying. Replace belts that have worn out. Multiple-belt arrangement needs careful evaluation to avoid risk of over loading one or more belts. 		L Semiannually
Check sheaves for proper alignment and excessive wear
Sheave misalignment can cause premature bearing/coupling failure, seal damage, increased vibration and noise.3 Shaft misalignment can be of three types: vertical angularity (twist), horizontal angularity and axial offset. Two most common methods of sheave alignment are straight edge and string method.Dust build up on a fan blade can reduce fan performance significantly. Dirt building up on motor housing insulates the motor and prevents it from operating at higher temperatures. Clean fan blades and motor housing periodically. Ensure power to the circuit breaker and other ancillary devices is turned off.94,5 		L Annually
Check bearings
Inadequate/over lubrication, normal fatigue and contamination are the main causes for bearing failures.7 Lookout for unusual bearing operation such as uneven running, reduced working accuracy, unusual running noises or any combination of the three.8 Evaluate cause of failure before installing new bearings. 		L Annually
Check fan blades and housing
Dust build up on a fan blade can reduce fan performance significantly. Dirt building up on motor housing insulates the motor and prevents it from operating at higher temperatures. Clean fan blades and motor housing periodically. Ensure power to the circuit breaker and other ancillary devices is turned off.9 		L Semiannually
Check VFD for proper operation
Although VFD’s are robust, they can sometimes be not operating as designed. As a preliminary step, check the controller for fault diagnostics, check the wiring for loose connections and identify overheated components (if any).10 		L Annually

Air distribution and Air handler Maintenance (ASHRAE Standard 180-2012)
Measure Level of Effort Frequency
Survey belt driven equipment and evaluate suitability and cost effectiveness of replacing existing V-belts with notched or synchronous belts
Notched belts run cooler, have longer durability compared to V belts and run with an efficiency of ~97%. Synchronous belts operate at ~98% efficiency over a wider load range, need minimal maintenance and can operate in wet/oily environments. However, they transfer more vibrations, are sometimes noisier than V-belts and less suited for shock-load applications.1,2,3 		L Monthly
Install VFD
Understand AHU operation and evaluate the max load and part load profile. If the application is not constant speed, evaluate retrofitting constant speed motors with a VFD. 4 		M Once
Apply measures listed in the general air handling unit checklist

Evaluate Fan and Motor Size
Measure Level of Effort Frequency
Check if fan motor is oversized
Fans end up being oversized while trying to account for a safety factor. A common indication of an oversized fan is when inlet vanes and dampers remain closed very often. Accurate estimation of oversizing requires detailed measurements to identify the operating point on the fan curve.1 An oversized impeller might turn out to be a good thing but an undersized impeller could be an opportunity for efficiency improvement. Consider adding a VFD and verifying fan and system operation before replacing it. 		M Once

“A U.S. Environmental Protection Agency (EPA) study found that almost 60 percent of building fan systems were oversized by at least 10 percent, with an average oversizing of 60 percent.”
Source: ENREGY STAR® Building Manual (Chapter 8- Air Distribution Systems)

System Measures
The fan system should be checked for proper operation. The following System Measures help improve fan system performance:
System Maintenance Items
Measure Level of Effort Frequency
Check and replace air filters
Loaded air filters lead to significant increase in system pressure and can cause instability problem. A differential pressure sensor can be installed across the filter bank to estimate accurate filter change out time.1 		L Per manufacturer’s recommendation or when differential pressure reading reaches the maximum threshold
Inspect and clean coils
Dirty coils reduce the heat transfer effectiveness, increasing the chiller energy consumption.2 Coil cleaning was reported to improve IAQ and HVAC system performance by ~10-15%.3 		L Every 6 months
Inspect and correct damper operations, including economizers
Dampers many a times get manually over-ridden and stay in the same position until inspected. Check whether outdoor damper and return dampers stroke freely with minimum play. Outdoor damper should be able to open 100% and return damper should close tightly. Ensure damper assembly is clean and clear of debris.4 		L-M Annually
Test, adjust, and balance fan systems
Perform a pre-balance equipment and system check. Compare installed equipment to those mentioned on the mechanical drawings. Have equipment and system balance done by a Testing, Adjusting and Balancing certified technician.5 		M As needed
Check and correct leakage of ducts, air system cabinet, and VAV boxes
Air leakage leads to poor indoor air quality, increased energy use and thermal comfort problems. Perform leakage tests to identify locations that need to be sealed.6 Refer to ANSI/ASHRAE standard 193: Method of test for determining the airtightness of HVAC equipment.7 		H Once
Check terminal units and diffusers for blockage
Check for books on ventilator cabinets, plastic or cardboard taped over diffusers, furniture covering floor and wall inlet/outlet grilles etc. Sometimes they indicate that people in that space have thermal comfort problems. Identify such problems and adjust the system as needed. 		L Annually

Verifying Proper Control as Designed
Measure Level of Effort Frequency
Check if fan can turn off when not needed
All fan motors should have controls that can turn off the fan when not needed. Control can be done based on an occupancy sensor or a timer.1 Avoid using “duty-cycling” approach that arbitrarily shuts down fans for short periods of time without taking into account the building’s HVAC requirement. Evaluate building pressurization requirements before turning off fans.3 		L Quarterly
Ensure variable speed settings are properly implemented
Fan motors greater or equal to 5HP should be provided with a VFD, and should be indicated on the mechanical plan.1 		L Quarterly
Check static pressure reset
Review duct static pressure trend to check if duct static pressure set point stays constant during times when fan load should have varied. Review damper positions to check if static pressure is too high or too low.2 Have the BAS monitor the duct static pressure trend to determine if a reset is possible. 		L Quarterly

Evaluate System Installation
Measure Level of Effort Frequency
Check facility operational requirements
Are the facility’s requirements the same? (occupancy patterns, internal loads, etc. may have changed since initial system design) Is the current system design still relevant? If not, what design changes are needed? Are there components installed in the system which were not considered in design? If yes, then identify components that are introducing significant pressure drop.1,2 		L-M Upon changes in building occupants, operating schedules, or substantial equipment installation/upgrades
